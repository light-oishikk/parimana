% ── Executive Summary ──────────────────────────────────
\section*{Executive Summary}

This report summarises progress during the first three weeks (1--20 May 2026) of the PARIMANA Summer Internship. The project aims to produce a structured survey of deep learning methods applied to quantum imaging reconstruction. During the reporting period, the scope was formalised across three principal imaging modalities---quantum ghost imaging, compressed sensing with entangled photons, and sub-shot-noise imaging---and a systematic literature search was conducted, yielding approximately 45 candidate papers. Detailed review of the ghost imaging domain has been completed, identifying that deep learning methods enable a 10--100$\times$ reduction in the number of measurements required for faithful image reconstruction compared to traditional correlation-based approaches. Work on compressed sensing and hybrid classical--quantum architectures is underway and proceeding on schedule.

% ── 1. Introduction ────────────────────────────────────
\section{Introduction and Project Objectives}

Quantum imaging exploits non-classical photon correlations---primarily generated via spontaneous parametric down-conversion (SPDC)---to achieve imaging capabilities that surpass classical limits in resolution, noise tolerance, and photon efficiency \citep{pittman1995, erkmen2010}. However, reconstructing high-quality images from the sparse, noisy measurements inherent to quantum optical systems remains a significant computational challenge.

Recent advances in deep learning have demonstrated remarkable success in solving inverse problems in computational imaging \citep{barbastathis2019, lecun2015}. This project surveys the intersection of these two fields, with the following objectives:

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
    \item Systematically catalogue deep learning architectures applied to quantum ghost imaging, compressed sensing with entangled photons, and sub-shot-noise imaging.
    \item Analyse hybrid classical--quantum reconstruction frameworks and their relative merits.
    \item Identify methodological trends, standardisation gaps, and open research problems.
    \item Propose directions for integrating quantum-inspired neural architectures into imaging pipelines.
\end{enumerate}

% ── 2. Methodology ─────────────────────────────────────
\section{Methodology}

\subsection{Systematic Search Strategy}
A structured literature search was conducted across four databases: IEEE Xplore, arXiv (categories \texttt{quant-ph}, \texttt{eess.IV}, \texttt{cs.CV}), Google Scholar, and Optica Publishing Group. Search queries combined terms from two groups:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Quantum imaging:} ``ghost imaging'', ``quantum imaging'', ``entangled photon imaging'', ``sub-shot-noise imaging'', ``SPDC imaging'', ``correlated photon''.
    \item \textbf{Deep learning:} ``deep learning'', ``neural network'', ``CNN'', ``GAN'', ``U-Net'', ``autoencoder'', ``image reconstruction'', ``compressed sensing''.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Inclusion and Exclusion Criteria}
Papers were included if they (a)~applied a deep learning or neural-network method to a quantum or quantum-inspired imaging reconstruction task, or (b)~introduced foundational theory directly underpinning such methods. Review articles and purely classical computational imaging works were included only where they provided essential context.

\subsection{Taxonomic Framework}
A three-axis classification was developed to organise the surveyed literature:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Imaging modality:} ghost imaging, quantum compressed sensing, sub-shot-noise imaging, hybrid systems.
    \item \textbf{DL architecture:} CNN, GAN, U-Net, autoencoder, fully connected, quantum-inspired.
    \item \textbf{Reconstruction task:} compressed reconstruction, denoising, super-resolution, phase retrieval.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Progress Summary}

\subsection{Week 1 (1--7 May): Scope and Literature Discovery}

\begin{itemize}
    \item Finalised the survey scope around three core imaging modalities and the cross-cutting theme of hybrid classical--quantum frameworks.
    \item Conducted the initial literature search, identifying \textbf{45 candidate papers} spanning foundational quantum optics, computational ghost imaging, and deep learning for inverse problems.
    \item Developed the three-axis taxonomic classification framework described in Section~2.3.
    \item Reviewed foundational works on quantum ghost imaging \citep{pittman1995, strekalov1995, bennink2002} and computational ghost imaging \citep{shapiro2008, ferri2005}.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Week 2 (8--14 May): Deep Learning for Ghost Imaging}

This week focused on a detailed review of deep learning methods applied to ghost imaging reconstruction:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{CNN-based methods:} Reviewed \citet{lyu2017}, the first demonstration of DL-based ghost imaging, which used a fully connected network to reconstruct $28 \times 28$ images at a 12.5\% sampling ratio. \citet{he2018} improved upon this with convolutional architectures showing better generalisation to unseen object categories.
    \item \textbf{Computational GI with DL:} \citet{shimobaba2018} applied deep learning specifically to computational ghost imaging, demonstrating improvement over traditional correlation-based reconstruction.
    \item \textbf{End-to-end approaches:} \citet{wang2019} introduced joint optimisation of illumination patterns and reconstruction networks, achieving 3--5\,dB PSNR improvement over methods that optimise these components separately.
    \item \textbf{Key finding:} DL methods consistently enable a \textbf{10--100$\times$ reduction} in the number of measurements required for faithful reconstruction compared to traditional correlation-based approaches.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Week 3 (15--20 May): Compressed Sensing and Hybrid Methods}

\begin{itemize}
    \item Reviewed compressive ghost imaging \citep{katz2009}, which first applied compressed sensing theory to ghost imaging, and its theoretical foundations \citep{donoho2006, candes2006}.
    \item Examined few-photon imaging with entangled states \citep{morris2015}, which motivates the use of DL denoising in quantum-limited regimes.
    \item Began reviewing quantum-inspired neural network architectures \citep{beer2020, killoran2019} for potential application to quantum image reconstruction.
    \item Started cross-referencing hybrid classical--quantum reconstruction frameworks across the surveyed domains.
\end{itemize}

% ── 4. Key Findings ────────────────────────────────────
\section{Key Findings to Date}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Measurement efficiency:} Deep learning methods reduce the number of required measurements by one to two orders of magnitude across all reviewed ghost imaging studies, with end-to-end learned approaches \citep{wang2019} providing the largest gains.

    \item \textbf{Architecture convergence:} The field is converging towards U-Net \citep{ronneberger2015} and GAN-based \citep{goodfellow2014} architectures for perceptually high-quality reconstruction, mirroring trends in classical computational imaging \citep{barbastathis2019}.

    \item \textbf{Quantum--classical boundary:} The transition from quantum to classical to computational ghost imaging \citep{erkmen2010} raises questions about when quantum resources provide genuine advantage; DL methods may narrow this gap further by compensating for classical noise.

    \item \textbf{Benchmarking deficit:} No standardised benchmark datasets or evaluation protocols exist across the reviewed papers, making direct comparison of methods difficult.
\end{enumerate}

% ── 5. Challenges ──────────────────────────────────────
\section{Challenges Encountered}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Interdisciplinary scope:} The survey spans quantum optics, information theory, and deep learning, requiring synthesis across communities with different conventions, notation, and publication venues.
    \item \textbf{Rapidly evolving field:} Several relevant preprints have appeared on arXiv during the survey period, necessitating ongoing monitoring.
    \item \textbf{Reproducibility:} Many experimental papers do not release code or datasets, complicating efforts to compare reported performance metrics.
    \item \textbf{Terminological inconsistency:} The terms ``quantum imaging'', ``ghost imaging'', and ``correlated imaging'' are used with varying specificity across the literature.
\end{itemize}
