\section{Introduction}

This report presents an interim summary of progress on the project titled \emph{``A Survey of Deep Learning-Based Reconstruction Methods for Quantum Imaging Systems,''} undertaken as part of the PARIMANA Summer Internship Program 2026 at Qmet Tech Foundation. The project aims to produce a systematic survey of deep learning architectures applied to quantum imaging reconstruction, with particular attention to ghost imaging, compressed sensing with entangled photons, and sub-shot-noise imaging. The study also examines hybrid classical--quantum frameworks and proposes directions for integrating quantum-inspired neural architectures into imaging pipelines.

The present document describes the research methodology adopted, summarises the key technical findings obtained thus far, and reports on the development of an accompanying software framework for experimental validation of the surveyed methods.

\section{Research Methodology}

A systematic literature review was conducted following established survey methodology. The search strategy targeted four primary databases: IEEE Xplore, arXiv (spanning the \texttt{quant-ph}, \texttt{eess.IV}, and \texttt{cs.CV} categories), Google Scholar, and the Optica Publishing Group digital library. Search queries were constructed by combining quantum imaging terminology---including ``ghost imaging,'' ``quantum imaging,'' ``entangled photon imaging,'' ``sub-shot-noise imaging,'' and ``SPDC''---with deep learning terms such as ``neural network,'' ``CNN,'' ``GAN,'' ``U-Net,'' and ``image reconstruction.'' Papers were included if they applied a deep learning method to a quantum or quantum-inspired imaging reconstruction task, or if they introduced foundational theory directly underpinning such methods.

To organise the collected literature, a three-axis taxonomic classification framework was developed. The first axis categorises papers by imaging modality: ghost imaging, quantum compressed sensing, sub-shot-noise imaging, or hybrid systems. The second axis classifies the deep learning architecture employed, distinguishing among convolutional neural networks, generative adversarial networks, U-Net variants, autoencoders, and quantum-inspired designs. The third axis captures the specific reconstruction task addressed, such as compressed reconstruction, denoising, super-resolution, or phase retrieval. This framework has proven effective for identifying methodological trends and gaps across the surveyed literature.

\section{Key Findings}

The literature review has yielded several findings of significance. In the domain of quantum ghost imaging, deep learning methods have been shown to reduce the number of required measurements by one to two orders of magnitude compared to traditional correlation-based reconstruction \citep{lyu2017, he2018, shimobaba2018}. Early work by \citet{lyu2017} demonstrated that a fully connected neural network could reconstruct ghost images at a sampling ratio of 12.5\%, while subsequent studies employing convolutional architectures achieved usable reconstruction at sampling ratios below 10\% \citep{he2018}. A particularly notable contribution is the end-to-end framework of \citet{wang2019}, which jointly optimises illumination patterns and the reconstruction network, yielding 3--5\,dB improvement in peak signal-to-noise ratio over methods that optimise these components independently.

The review has also identified a clear convergence in the field towards U-Net \citep{ronneberger2015} and GAN-based \citep{goodfellow2014} architectures, mirroring broader trends in computational imaging \citep{barbastathis2019}. GAN-based approaches produce perceptually sharper reconstructions but introduce a risk of hallucination---generating plausible but incorrect image features---which is a concern in scientific imaging contexts where fidelity to the true object is essential.

In the area of compressed sensing with quantum correlations, the foundational work of \citet{katz2009} demonstrated that ghost imaging can be naturally formulated as a compressed sensing problem, exploiting image sparsity to achieve sub-Nyquist reconstruction. The theoretical underpinnings of this approach, grounded in the restricted isometry property \citep{donoho2006, candes2006}, are well established, and recent efforts have begun combining compressed sensing structure with learned neural network priors for further improvement.

Sub-shot-noise imaging, first demonstrated experimentally by \citet{brida2010} and extended to wide-field microscopy by \citet{samantaray2017}, represents another frontier where deep learning denoising may extend the practical window of quantum advantage. The investigation of hybrid classical--quantum architectures, including quantum-inspired neural networks \citep{beer2020} and continuous-variable quantum neural networks \citep{killoran2019}, is also underway.

A recurring observation across the surveyed literature is the absence of standardised benchmarks: different studies employ different image sizes, datasets, noise models, and evaluation metrics, which makes rigorous cross-comparison of methods difficult. This benchmarking deficit has been identified as a key open problem that the survey will address in its discussion.

\section{Software Development}

To complement the literature survey and provide empirical grounding for the reviewed methods, a software framework has been developed in Python using PyTorch. The framework comprises three principal components.

The first component is a computational ghost imaging simulator that implements the forward measurement model, including configurable illumination pattern generation (Gaussian, binary, and Hadamard bases), bucket detector signal computation, and traditional correlation-based reconstruction following \citet{erkmen2010}. This simulator enables controlled experimentation with different measurement parameters, sampling ratios, and noise levels.

The second component consists of implementations of three representative deep learning architectures drawn from the surveyed literature: a fully connected reconstruction network modelled after \citet{lyu2017}, a convolutional architecture inspired by \citet{he2018}, and a U-Net variant adapted for ghost imaging reconstruction. These implementations allow direct comparison of the architectural approaches discussed in the survey.

The third component provides training and evaluation infrastructure, including standard image quality metrics (PSNR, SSIM), automated training loops with validation, and visualisation utilities for comparing reconstructions across methods. Preliminary experiments using MNIST digit images confirm that the deep learning models achieve substantially higher reconstruction quality than the traditional correlation method at equivalent sampling ratios, consistent with the findings reported in the literature.

\section{Summary}

The project is progressing on schedule. The systematic literature review has covered the primary domains of ghost imaging and compressed sensing, with sub-shot-noise imaging and hybrid architectures currently under review. Key findings regarding measurement efficiency, architectural trends, and benchmarking gaps have been identified and are being incorporated into the survey manuscript. The accompanying software framework is operational and producing results consistent with the published literature. The survey document is being compiled concurrently and will be finalised during the remaining period of the internship.
